Take Five
=========

Composer: Paul Desmond
Performed by: The Dave Brubeck Quartet
Year: 1959 (from the album "Time Out")
Status: COPYRIGHTED — placeholder. Purchase or borrow legally.

Key: Eb minor
Time: 5/4 (THIS is the song's whole personality)
Tempo: ~174 bpm (feels mid-tempo because of the 5/4 grouping)
Difficulty: The piano comp on the original recording is surprisingly simple —
            just two chords vamping. The challenge is FEELING the 5/4. Worth
            the work for an advanced beginner who wants a "wow" piece.

Why it belongs here
-------------------
- The most famous song in 5/4 time, period
- Brubeck's piano part is basically two chords (Ebm and Bbm7) alternating in a
  syncopated pattern — way more approachable than people assume
- Playing in 5/4 is a great rhythmic workout that will improve every other tune
  you play afterward
- One of the highest-selling jazz singles ever — instant audience recognition

The 5/4 trick
-------------
Count it as "ONE-two-three / ONE-two" — a 3+2 grouping, not 1-2-3-4-5.
That's why the piano vamp lands where it does. Say it out loud while you play:
"BOM-bom-bom-CHIK-chik / BOM-bom-bom-CHIK-chik"

Practice tips
-------------
1. Get the 5/4 in your body FIRST — clap or tap it for a few minutes before
   touching the keys. Most people fail at this song because they never feel 5.
2. Learn the LH vamp alone. It's just Ebm to Bbm7 in syncopated rhythm.
   Should be automatic before you add the melody.
3. The melody is the famous part — sing it before you try to play it
4. Use a metronome set to 5/4 (or set to a slow 5 quarter-note clicks)
5. Reference recording: Dave Brubeck Quartet, "Time Out" (1959, Columbia)
   — listen 5 times before playing once
